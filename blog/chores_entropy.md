# Chores and the Second Law of Thermodynamics

The next time you are doing household chores, take a moment to think about how they make you feel. Rank the different tasks based on your preference — my observations are below, and I’d be keen to know if you agree.

I’ve been lucky enough to work from home for most of 2020, largely uninterrupted — my role doesn’t require being in the office or a specific location [1]. All I really need is a laptop and a stable internet connection. There have been numerous perks (and downsides), but one unexpected benefit has been a newfound appreciation for household chores.

In a pre-Covid world, chores were — true to their definition — a necessary evil. After a long day at the office, with a commute on either side, the last thing anyone wants to do is wash dishes or do laundry. Naturally, these tasks get pushed to the weekend. And the weekend, ideally, is for relaxing or socialising — “I can’t come to the pub because I need to vacuum the flat” isn’t an argument that holds up for long.

Over time, I started interleaving chores into my workday. A quick break to load the dishwasher or hang up clothes became something I (almost) looked forward to. It’s procrastination at its finest — productive, but not quite “work” (although perhaps it should be work, without the quotes [2]).

With this shift, I also started analysing chores more closely. How long do they take? When should I do them? I found myself blocking out calendar time between meetings for these tasks. Combined with breaks for cooking, eating, and exercise, it makes for a surprisingly structured day.

## Chores and Entropy

Through this process, I started noticing patterns. Chores feel different — at least to me — because they reverse entropy to varying degrees.

Entropy can be thought of as the level of disorder in a system. The Second Law of Thermodynamics, loosely speaking, states that entropy in a closed system always increases [3]. In other words, things naturally move from order to chaos [4].

Household chores are, at their core, a small but persistent fight against this tendency [5].

Take laundry as an example. Moving clothes from the basket to the washing machine doesn’t meaningfully reduce entropy — it’s still a pile of disorder. Unsurprisingly, this is one of my least favourite steps.

By contrast, hanging clothes to dry transforms a tangled mess into something ordered and structured. There is a visible improvement, and with it, a sense of satisfaction. It feels like a small win in an otherwise losing battle.

Henry Adams captures this well: “Chaos was the law of nature; Order was the dream of man.” [6]

## A Simple Heuristic

The more a chore reduces entropy, the more satisfying it feels.

That observation loosely translates into my personal ranking:

### Laundry
1. Hanging clothes to dry — _large decrease in entropy_  
2. Folding clothes — _ordered to ordered_  
3. Loading the washing machine — _disordered to disordered_  

### Dishes
1. Loading the dishwasher — _messy sink to structured racks_  
2. Washing remaining items by hand — _similar outcome, more effort_  
3. Unloading the dishwasher — _ordered to ordered_  

### Cleaning
1. Vacuuming / general cleaning — _visible transformation, high payoff_  

## Reflection

Working from home versus returning to the office remains a debated topic. But one thing is certain — I’ll miss having the flexibility to interleave chores into my day.

It turns out that even small, mundane tasks can be surprisingly satisfying, provided they move things — however slightly — from chaos towards order.
