# 75 Hard

I recently completed 75 Hard.

For those unfamiliar, it is a mental conditioning programme built around a strict set of daily tasks, completed for 75 consecutive days — no breaks, no exceptions, and limited subjectivity [1].

## The Rules

The core requirements are:

- Two 45-minute workouts per day (one outdoors)
- Follow a nutrition plan (no cheat days)
- Drink 1 gallon (~4L) of water
- No alcohol
- Read 10 pages of non-fiction
- Take a daily progress picture

I added a few additional habits:

- 5 minutes of meditation (Headspace)
- Close the Stand Ring on Apple Fitness
- Avoid using the lift in my apartment
- Attempt the daily NYT Wordle

Missing any of the core tasks meant restarting from Day 1.

## Approach

The biggest source of ambiguity is diet. To minimise subjectivity, I chose intermittent fasting (15:9). Rather than debating what constitutes a “cheat meal”, the rule became time-based: eat within the window, fast outside it.

This made adherence simpler and more objective.

## Why Do It?

The timing was not accidental.

I had just returned from an extended period in India — typically associated (for me, at least) with a more sedentary lifestyle and higher calorie intake. Add to that weddings, social events, and a routine involving frequent drinking and limited exercise.

The goal was to reset — physically and mentally.

## The Experience

Some days felt mechanical — a checklist to complete. Others were genuinely enjoyable.

Tracking progress was a surprisingly strong motivator. The act of ticking off tasks, particularly starting the fasting window, reinforced discipline.

There were also obvious downsides:

- Staying up late to finish water intake  
- Late-night workouts after social events  
- Exercising outdoors in consistently poor London weather  

Adaptation became necessary. For example, incorporating walking into my commute to complete outdoor sessions early.

## The Outcome

The most meaningful result was not physical.

It was the experience of committing to something difficult and seeing it through — without resetting.

That sense of discipline extended beyond the programme. Decision-making became more direct, with less hesitation.

There were physical benefits — weight loss and improved definition — but these felt secondary.

Another notable outcome was social: learning to engage fully without alcohol. In a culture where drinking is often central, this was both challenging and useful.

## Sustainability

The full programme is not designed to be maintained indefinitely.

Some elements are actively counterproductive long-term. For example, 4L of water daily exceeds typical recommendations when accounting for all fluid sources [2].

The challenge is to extract the useful components and build a sustainable system:

- Reduce water intake to ~2–3L  
- Remove constraints on reading content  
- Introduce flexibility (e.g. structured “off” days)  

The goal shifts from rigidity to consistency.

## Reflection

75 Hard is not inherently special. Its value lies in forcing discipline through simplicity and constraint.

The key takeaway is not the programme itself, but the process of finding a balance that is both challenging and sustainable.
