# 75 Hard

Mission accomplished! I am thrilled to share I have successfully completed 75 Hard. For those who haven't heard of it, 75 Hard is a mental conditioning and strengthening challenge [1] with a host of other benefits such as improved discipline and physical health. It involves completing a series of tasks for 75 consecutive days - no breaks, no pauses and crucially, limited subjectivity. No excuses!

The original list is
* Doing two 45min workouts, one of which **must** be outdoors
* Following a nutrition plan of your choice - no cheat days
* Drinking 1 gallon of direct water (that's about 4l for us British folk)
* No drinking alcohol
* Reading 10 pages of a non-fiction self-help book
* Taking a progress picture

I added a few more to the list, mainly since I was already doing them daily and they fit the self-improvement theme nicely
* Meditating for 5min (Headspace)
* Closing the Stand Ring on Apple Fitness
* Not using the lift in my apartment block
* Attempting the daily NYT Wordle

Two key points: missing _any_ item on the first list would reset to Day 1, and I had to be honest with myself. There is room for subjectivity, e.g. around the nutrition plan or what really constitutes a workout. I decided to pick Intermittent Fasting (IF) with a generous 15-9 schedule to mitigate this - there is less subjectivity on clock times than "does this count as a cheat food". In effect, I could eat anything I wanted but only during the non-fasting window. Ultimately, this is about being accountable to myself.

The most common question I get is why I would willingly put myself through something like this... there are a few reasons. I had just spent nearly three months in India prior to starting - it generally leads to a sedentary lifestyle with excessive eating. I had also attended 2 weddings in that period, which are high calorific affairs with an easy excuse of "it's a wedding". Even aside from India, I was stuck in a routine at work with fairly heavy social drinking and limited exercise. The aim was to shock the body into resetting and inculcating some healthy habits. My friends had good success with 75 Hard the year prior and I saw first-hand the benefits it brought.

Accidentally, I found myself doing 75 Hard at a very good time of the year - I kicked off in late April, a day after my parents left for home (they had been visiting me in London). It gave me Spring to reset and hit Summer with good habits. On the 76th day, I was running the JP Morgan Corporate Challenge, a 5.6km run through Battersea Park with colleagues - a great chance to end on a high. More importantly, the post-run dinner and drinks at the Magic Garden is the stuff of legends. A hidden gem, a pub with a lovely beer garden, it would make the perfect backdrop for my first celebratory drink in 75 days. And I had a friend's wedding the weekend after for more enjoyment - I couldn't have timed it better!

Some days, the checklist felt exactly like that - a tick-box exercise with little motivation, just something to get done. Other days, I would start strong and actually look forward to completing everything on the list. The physical act of tracking the list on my phone, ticking items as done and counting down the days to go was a great motivator. It was particularly noticeable for tracking IF - the act of marking the start of my fasting window was a clear signal to the brain that I was not going to eat further that day.

Of course, there are downsides - I found myself doing stupid things like staying up an extra hour at night just to drink the daily quota of water. Or worse, going for workouts at 2am or 3am if I had a late evening out. And don't forget, I live in London - a 75 day period without continuous rain would be unheard of [2]. Exercising 45min in the rain is an inevitability for anyone taking on the challenge in the UK. I did get creative, e.g. leaving home early and walking 3 stops along my tube route before starting my commute. Getting one of the workouts, preferably the outdoor one, done before work was a bonus.

The biggest win? It's the feeling of committing to something and getting it done (especially without resetting, which was a common pitfall for others taking the challenge). The feeling of having achieved something difficult and the satisfaction it brings is indescribable. As I had hoped, these qualities extended to my non-75 Hard activities - there is a very real feeling of "if I can do this, I can do anything". I have also become more direct in conversations at work or with friends, less faffing about. I did lose weight, and the improvement to body definition was noticeable - strangely though, I consider these side benefits rather than the main goal of the challenge.

Another positive was proving to myself I could socialise with friends _and_ have a good time without drinking alcohol - especially in the UK, where going for a quick pint at the pub is a staple. Doing 75 Hard is an easy out from peer pressure, particularly in later stages once you are fully committed. One of my early challenges (Days 15-17) was a Sten (combined Stag + Hen) in Alicante. Somehow, I didn't succumb to peer pressure for drinking _while_ maintaining the challenge. A big shoutout to my friends who didn't pressure me beyond the gentle ribbing that comes with any friendship. And kudos to a couple of them who even joined me on the workouts while continuing to drink and party late - that deserves a special mention!

What next? Carrying on the full list on a daily basis is futile and provides limited value - it very much dominates your calendar and impacts your ability to fully enjoy life. Moreover, some of the items on the list are actively harmful if done daily beyond 75 days. Daily recommended water intake is about 1 gallon from _all_ sources [3], which likely means closer to 2-3l from direct water, allowing for tea/coffee, juices and other sources. 4l per day can be harmful if continued indefinitely! And then there is the psychological impact of not having cheat days or breaks, with a common criticism of 75 Hard being the reset to Day 1 leading to perceiving oneself as a failure.

I am on the hunt for a sustainable balance - something that is challenging enough to feel worthy of achievement, but that doesn't feel excessive. There are some easy simplifications - bringing the water down to 2-3l per above is a start. As someone who enjoys reading for pleasure anyway, another change could be dropping the "non-fiction / self-help" aspect of the 10 pages reading a day. Introducing a mechanism for cheat days can help make a rigid fitness programme more sustainable - e.g. not missing 2 days in a row, or having a punishment if you do, like a longer workout the following day. Although there is also research to suggest the feeling of guilt associated with a 'cheat' meal is exactly what we are trying to avoid! [4] The idea is to build a healthy and sustainable habit without punishing a bad day which every human has.

Ultimately, it's about finding the right balance for yourself - there is no one right answer! A big thank you to everyone - friends, family and colleagues - who accommodated changes to my lifestyle over the 75 days and put up with my patterns. It wouldn't have been possible without you!
