Mission accomplished! I am thrilled to announce I have successfully completed 75 Hard. For those who haven't heard of it, 75 Hard is a mental conditioning and strengthening challenge [1] with other benefits such as physical health improvements. It involves completing a series of tasks for 75 consecutive days - no breaks, no pauses and crucially, limited subjectivity. No excuses!

The original list is
* Two 45min workouts, one of which **must** be outdoors
* Follow a nutrition plan of your choice
* Drink 1 gallon of water (that's about 4l for us British folk)
* No drinking alcohol
* Read 10 pages of a non-fiction self-help book
* Take a progress picture

I added a few more to the list, mainly since I was already doingt them daily and they fit the self-improvement theme nicely
* Meditate for 5min (Headspace)
* Close the Stand Ring on Apple Fitness
* No using the lift in my apartment block
* Attempt the daily NYT Wordle

Two key points - missing _any_ item on the first list would reset to Day 1. And I had to be honest with myself. There is room for subjectivity, e.g. around the nutrition plan or what really constitutes a workout. Ultimately, this is about being accountable to myself. I decided to pick Intermittent Fasting (IF) with a generous 15-9 schedule to mitigate this - there is less subjectivity on clock times than "does this count as a cheat food". In effect, I could eat anything I wanted but only during the non-fasting window.

The most common question I get is why I would willingly put myself through something like this... there are a few different reasons. I had just spent nearly three months in India prior to starting - it generally leads to a sedantary lifestyle with excessive eating. I also attended 2 weddings in that period, which are high calorific affairs with an easy excuse of "it's a wedding". Even aside from India, I was stuck in a routine at work with fairly heavy social drinking and limited exercise. The aim was to shock the body into resetting and inculcating some healthy habits. My friends had good success with 75 Hard the year prior and I saw first hand the benefits it brought.

Accidentally, I found myself doing 75 Hard at a very good time of the year - I kicked off in late April, a day after my parents left (they had been visiting me in London). It gave me Spring to reset and hit Summer with good habits. On the 76th day, I was running the JP Morgan Corporate Challenge, a 5.6km run through Battersea Park with colleagues - a great chance to end on a high. More importantly, the post run dinner and drinks at the Magic Garden is the stuff of legends. A hidden gem, a pub with a lovely beer garden, it would make the perfect background for my first celebratory drink in 75 days. And I had a friend's wedding the weekend after for more enjoyment - I couldn't have timed it better!

Some days, the checklist felt exactly like that - a tickbox exercise with little motivation, just something to get done. Other days, I would start strong and actually look forward to completing everything on the list. The physical act of tracking the list on my phone, ticking items as done and counting down the days to go was a great motivator. It was particularly noticable for tracking IF - the act of marking the end of my fasting window was a clear signal to the brain that I was not going to eat further that day.

Of course, there are downsides - I was doing stypid things like staying up an extra hour at night just to drink my daily quota of water. Or worse, going for workouts at 2am or 3am if I had a late evening out. And don't forget, I live in London - a 75 day period without continous rain would be unheard of. So walking 45min in the rain is an inevitablity for anyone taking on the challenge. I did get creative, e.g. leaving home early and walking 3 stops along my tube route before starting my commute - getting one of the workouts, preferably the outdoor one, done before work was a huge win.

The biggest win? It's the feeling of committing to something and getting it done (especially without resetting, which was a common pitfall for others taking the challenge). The feeling of having achieved something, the satisfaction it brings is indescribable. As I had hoped, these qualities extended to my non-75 Hard activities - I have become more direct in conversations at work or with friends, less faffing about. There is a very real feeling of "if I can do this, I can do anything". Yes I did lose weight, and the physical body improvements were noticable - strangely though, I consider these side benfits rather than the main goal of the challenge.

Another positive was proving to myself I could socialise with friends _and_ have a good time without drinking - especially in the UK, where going for a quick one at the pub is a staple. Doing 75 Hard is an easy out from peer pressure, particularly in later stages - one of my early challenges (Days 15-17) was a Sten (combined Stag + Hen) Do in Alicante. Somehow, I didn't succumb to peer pressure for drinking _while_ maintaining the challenge. A big shoutout to my friends who didn't pressure me beyond the gentle ribbing that comes with any friendship! And kudos to a couple of them who even joined me on the workouts despite drinking and partying, that deserves a special mention.

What next? I think carrying on the full list on a daily basis is futile and provides limited value - it very much dominates your calendar and impacts your ability to fully enjoy life. Moreover, some of the items on the list are actively harmful if done daily beyond 75 Days. And there is the psychological impact of not having cheat days or breaks, with a common criticism being the reset to Day 1 leading to perceving oneself as a failure.

I am on the hunt for a sustainable balance - something that is challenging enough to feel worthy of achievement, but that doesn't feel excessive. There are some easy simplifications - four litres of water per day is harmful if continued indefinitely. Daily recommendations vary between two and three litres, an easy adjustment. As someone who enjoys reading for pleasure anyway, another easy simplification would be dropping the "non-fiction / self-help" aspect of the 10 pages reading a day. And introducing a mechanism for cheat days is proven to help make any fitness programme more sustainable - e.g. not missing 2 days in a row, or having a punishment if you do, like a longer workout the following day. The idea is to build healthy and sustainable habits without punishing a bad day which every human has.

Ultimately, it's about being honest with yourself - integrity is defined as "doing the right thing, even when no one is watching" - that sums up nicely how one should approach measuring progress towards 75 Hard. A big thank you to everyone, friends, family and colleagues, who accommodated these changes to my lifestyle over the 75 days. It wouldn't have been possible without you!
