# Nutella and Office Buildings

What do jars of Nutella™ and office buildings have in common?

It sounds like the start of a bad joke, but the connection is real: waste. The wastage of anything — however small — is something I find difficult to ignore. I’m clearly not alone in this [1], but I hadn’t fully appreciated the scale of the problem until looking into it more closely.

## Packaging and Everyday Waste

Take Nutella as an example.

Why is the jar designed such that the final portion at the bottom is difficult to access? And, as a minor but related irritation, why is the foil seal so awkward to peel? A small design tweak — an extended tab, perhaps — would make a noticeable difference. Yes, there are workarounds (use a knife, or your nails), but that misses the point.

For a global brand, small usability improvements should matter.

More broadly, food packaging often feels inherently wasteful.

Some people — myself included — go to great lengths to extract every last bit from a container. There are even specialised tools [3], or creative techniques [4], to minimise waste. But for most products — ketchup, honey, mayonnaise — the majority of people simply discard the remainder.

In some cases, it’s not even clear how much is left. With opaque packaging, like a mayonnaise bottle, the residual amount is effectively invisible [5].

## The Scale of the Problem

The impact is not trivial.

Estimates suggest that between 15% and 25% of a container’s contents may be wasted due to inaccessibility [6][1]. Combined with losses in the manufacturing process — reportedly around 200 million gallons annually [7] — the numbers become significant.

It is enough to justify research into improved packaging, including “no-stick” surfaces designed to minimise residue [7]. The underlying physics is surprisingly interesting and well explained in [1].

## Frugality vs Waste

One interpretation of this behaviour is simple frugality.

I grew up in Mumbai, where the phrase “paisa vasool” loosely translates to “value for money”. If I pay for 400g of Nutella, I expect to consume 400g — not 395g with the remainder discarded.

There is undoubtedly a financial component, but the stronger motivation is reducing waste itself. This extends beyond food, into areas where there is no direct personal cost.

## Office Buildings

Office buildings are a good example.

Living near Canary Wharf, I regularly pass large commercial buildings at various times of day. Fully lit, multiple televisions running on every floor, often with minimal occupancy.

Even before Covid, this level of usage felt excessive. In a world where offices operate at reduced capacity, it is harder to justify.

There are, of course, safety requirements — emergency lighting, for example — but that does not explain the full extent of consumption. Reducing unnecessary usage here would have a meaningful impact [8][9].

## A Broader Pattern

We live in a world of abundance. There is always more available, provided you are willing to pay for it.

The prevailing mindset tends towards replacement over repair, convenience over efficiency.

Yet, as a planet, we are already over-consuming. Current estimates suggest we require 1.78 Earths to sustain our lifestyle [10].

Nutella jars and office buildings are small examples, but they reflect a broader pattern. The issue is not just the waste itself, but the mindset that allows it.

That is the part worth questioning.
